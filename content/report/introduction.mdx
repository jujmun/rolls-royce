## Introduction

Our system is an sCO₂ Brayton cycle with MOF beds integrated into the air-cooling system for DAC. Its parasitic load is 5%: 0.5% for cooling and 4.5% for DAC, which captures 1100 t-CO₂/year in addition to ~70k tonnes displaced by selling nuclear rather than coal-generated electricity.

This project treats the HTTR as a commercial power plant: having a carbon-reduction system complementary to a primary electricity-generation role is more challenging and applicable to other reactors. Our approach was based on synergies: was there anything in the power plant to exploit for carbon-reduction?

The two most expensive parts of DAC are pulling air through a sorbent and regenerating the sorbent. Our Brayton cycle already pulls air in for cooling, and sorbents like MOFs can be regenerated with waste heat from the Brayton cycle. The air-in-anyway argument is partially flawed as the pressure-drop across the MOF-beds is much greater than across the heat-exchanger, so there are no OPEX benefits, but the CAPEX benefit of a pre-existing large air-blower remains. The regeneration point is valid.
