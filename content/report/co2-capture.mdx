## 3. CO₂ Capture

### 3.1 Why MOFs?

MOFs are nano-scale lattices with metal-ion nodes and organic linkers. Physisorption is used rather than chemisorption (as in amine-scrubbing), so they can be regenerated with low-temperature waste heat.

### 3.2 System structure

DAC system: air passes through a MOF bed, then cools the Brayton cycle in HX2. When one bed is full, flow diverts to the other while the first is regenerated with post-HX2a hot air.

### 3.3 % CO₂ captured

Breakthrough-curve data (e.g. Airthena MOF) scaled to our mass-flow rate. Mean capture rate ~25%; capture-per-pass 10–50% is typical for DAC.

### 3.4 Regeneration

Typical regeneration energies 2–10 MJ/kg-CO₂; we assumed 6 MJ/kg-CO₂. TVSA (75–120 °C, 10s–100s mbar). We use indirect conductive heating: hot air heats metal filaments in the MOF supports; air never contacts the MOF. Fixed beds (not rotating) due to long regeneration times.

### 3.5 Pressure drop across the bed

Estimated 2 kPa for laminate bed; 1.34 m/s velocity, ~1.8 m length, ~10×10 m cross-section (parallelised modules). Blower efficiency 80%.
